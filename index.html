What motivates me to work on Web Accessibility?

A colleague once surprised me with a sticker from a conference because she knew I was passionate about accessibility. The sticker read 'accessibility is usability'. That really sums it up for me. Good user experience and design is delightful and can be applied to any product design. Accordingly, I understand accessibility as a crucial part of inclusive design, aiming to make products usable for a broad range of users, for example to those living with disabilities. Furthermore, it encompasses a wide range of living conditions, from language and cultural backgrounds to access to digital tools and economical and environmental factors. When I look around, I see accessibility and usability everywhere, immediate and simple examples are building architecture and city planning to busses and sidewalks.

In our digital age, it’s essential to create products that are accessible to as many people as possible. As a Frontend Developer, I consider web accessibility for individuals with disabilities to be essential in my work. My focus has been on making web products accessible according to web standards. This ensures that many people, including those who rely on assistive technologies, can browse and use websites easily and independently.

I find making digital experiences accessible both creative and challenging. Sometimes, it’s as simple as implementing basic standards like semantic HTML or a well-structured document outline. While a product can be bluntly compliant with standards, it could still bare barriers. That’s where the beauty of expertise and commitment to creating inclusive experiences comes into play.

Accessibility has become the center of my career path and professional interest. It brings me joy to contribute my best to building accessible products and sharing knowledge to others interested in this field. It’s so fundamental, simply fair and makes people happy to experience accessible tools that add to their life, in best case delightfully.

Assistive technologies I am experienced with

As a Frontend Developer and Mac user, I’ve gained extensive hands-on familiarity with native Voiceover for both Mac and iOS platforms in various browser combinations, such as Safari, Chrome or Firefox.

While I’m more familiar with Voiceover on Mac, I’ve also tested with JAWS and Narrator on Windows. Setting up and using this screen reader on my virtual machine was a bit trickier for me, especially coming from an Apple OS background. When testing with JAWS and Narrator, I tend to stick to Chrome and Edge since it’s a popular screen reader and browser combination.

I’m aware that different screen readers interact with browser accessibility APIs in varying ways, leading users to have preferred combinations. For instance, some users opt for JAWS with Chrome or Edge, NVDA with Firefox or Chrome, Voiceover with Safari and Narrator with Edge. Additionally, I find screen reader cheat sheets handy for quick keyboard shortcuts reminders when needed. In general, I would advocate to test with at least two different screen readers to ensure broader accessibility. Also, testing with one native screen reader is better than not testing at all.

I’ve thoroughly browsed and tested entire websites, components, and special widgets to ensure screen reader usability, aligning them with WCAG standards and W3C’s design patterns and ARIA authoring practices. I typically test linear content in browse mode but also proficiently navigate non-linearly using shortcuts to browse by headlines, landmarks, lists, links, and more.

To ensure comprehensive testing, I use both automated tools and manual checks. This includes navigating and testing for keyboard-only browsing and examining various aspects like page zoom, focus flow, content outline, color contrast, and more.
